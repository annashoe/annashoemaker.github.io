Iron is one of the most abundant redox-active elements in our solar system, and under anoxic conditions microorganisms are capable of utilizing the reduction of Fe(III) to Fe(II) to fuel life processes. Abundant, active, and diverse microbiomes exist beneath ice masses, in often anoxic conditions, and are s upported by mineral-based energy made available through the communition of bedrock. Biologically available iron has been demonstrated through rock crushing experiments and through examining of trace elements from subglacial runoff. Culturing experiments with subglacial microbes have shown iron reduction using substrates such as lactate and yeast, which are either chemically undefined or not commonly found in subglacial environments. Substrates, such as H2 have been demonstrated to be available in subglacial environments through rock crushing experiments using glacial geology. Recent research has shown the capability of organisms from Icelandic basaltic glacial sediments, to use the redox couple Fe(III)/H2 for growth at subglacially relevant temperatures. However, it remains unknown how widespread this metabolic capability is in subglacial systems on Earth. This leads to the overarching hypothesis for this project:

Iron reduction is an important process that occurs in subglacial sediments through microbial activity utilizing either H2 or organic acids as the redox pairing.

The research will examine iron reduction at low temperatures using subglacial sediments from five glaciers with broad geographic distribution, using a combination of enrichment culturing and genomic analysis on subglacial sediments from a range of Alpine and Polar glaciers that are underlain by diverse bedrock substrates. Water is required for life and at the present time the only water on the surface of Mars is in the form of ice. Mars has hundreds of geomorphic features that are attributed to glaciation including lobate convex surface features, known as viscous flow features and lobate debris aprons. Mars also has abundant iron-bearing minerals, including Fe(III) minerals detected through spectroscopy and ground based missions, largely as anhydrous nanophase ferric oxides. Exploring how iron reduction in the cold dark sediments from beneath glaciers on Earth can support microbial activity provides a guide to the potential for similar processes that may have occurred in glaciated, iron-rich Martian terrain.
