Characterizing microbial life of subglacial drainage

When beginning the search for life beyond Earth one of the most important conditions that must be met is the presence of liquid water. Because of this, icy moons and ocean worlds have become the predominant target when searching for off-Earth life in our own solar system. One of these icy moons can be found orbiting Jupiter, called Europa. Europa is host to an expansive and salty ocean underneath an ice shell.

This along with its silicate mantle is considered to be able to cause initiation and maintaining of life. But how do we know what to look for when we send spacecraft to Europa? We can get pointed in the right direction by studying places on Earth that are like what is observed in space—called analogues. Once we understand our analogues we can go to astronomical bodies and:

1. Learn geochemistry and minerology

2. Infer biology

3. Inform what biosignatures to seek

Our analogue: The Devon Ice Cap

Two “hypersaline,” or very salty, lakes were recently discovered beneath the summit of the Devon Ice Cap, located just west of Greenland (Rutishauser et al., 2018). Information was gathered about the lakes using airborne radar measurements. With this information, certain conditions of the lakes could be determined using modeling techniques. The temperature of these salty lakes was determined to be no higher than 10.5°C.

Normally a lake would be frozen at much warmer temperatures, since the freezing point of water is 0°C. As water gets saltier, it can stay liquid at colder and colder temperatures. Because these lakes are still liquid, and at such cold temperatures, it’s extremely likely that they are hypersaline.

The microbes that could be in these lakes, like bacteria and other small forms of life, would likely have been growing and changing in isolation for the last 120,000 years.
